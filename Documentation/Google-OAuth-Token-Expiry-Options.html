Fixing the 7-Day Refresh Token Expiry

Two permanent options to avoid having to regenerate the Google OAuth refresh token every 7 days.

Why does the token expire every 7 days?

Google limits refresh token lifetimes for applications in Testing mode that use sensitive scopes (Calendar and Gmail). This is a safety measure — Google wants you to either verify your app with them or confirm it is only for internal use within your own organisation. Until you do one of those two things, every refresh token issued expires after 7 days, after which the server will fail to send calendar invites or password reset emails until the token is replaced.

Option 1 — Google Workspace Recommended

Google Workspace is Google's paid business suite (formerly G Suite). If your practice uses a custom domain (e.g. yourpractice.com), you can create a Workspace account for the platform email address (e.g. appointments@yourpractice.com). Workspace accounts can set the OAuth app to Internal user type, which means the app is restricted to your own organisation and Google treats it as pre-trusted — refresh tokens never expire.

What it costs

Google Workspace Business Starter starts at approximately $6 USD per user per month (pricing varies by region and billing cycle). You only need one account — the platform sending account. You do not need Workspace accounts for your therapists or clients.

What changes

  • The platform email becomes appointments@yourpractice.com instead of a Gmail address.
  • Calendar invites and password reset emails come from your own domain — more professional for clients.
  • The OAuth app user type is switched from External to Internal.
  • Refresh tokens issued to Internal apps never expire (unless you revoke them manually).

Step-by-step setup

1
Sign up for Google Workspace

Go to workspace.google.com and sign up. During setup you will be asked to verify ownership of your domain — Google will guide you through adding a DNS TXT record with your domain registrar. This typically takes 15–30 minutes.

Create a user account for the platform email address, e.g. appointments@yourpractice.com.

2
Sign in to Google Cloud Console with the new Workspace account

Go to console.cloud.google.com and sign in as appointments@yourpractice.com. You can either create a new project here or transfer your existing project to this account.

If you want to reuse your existing project, go to IAM & Admin → Settings in the Cloud Console and add the Workspace account as an owner, then switch to that account.
3
Switch the OAuth consent screen user type to Internal
APIs & Services›OAuth consent screen›Audience
  1. Open OAuth consent screen → Audience.
  2. Under User type, change External to Internal.
  3. Save.
Internal apps do not require Google verification and their refresh tokens never expire. The option to switch to Internal only appears when you are signed in with a Google Workspace account. It is not available for personal Gmail accounts.
4
Regenerate the refresh token once using the new account

Follow steps 11–15 from the main setup guide, signing in as appointments@yourpractice.com this time. The new refresh token will not expire.

Update the GOOGLE_REFRESH_TOKEN and GMAIL_FROM environment variables with the new values and restart both services.

After this setup: the refresh token is permanent. No action is needed unless you intentionally revoke it or rotate credentials. Calendar invites and password reset emails will come from appointments@yourpractice.com.

Option 2 — Google OAuth App Verification

Google allows any app — including those using a personal Gmail account — to have non-expiring refresh tokens once the app has been formally reviewed and verified by Google. This is a manual review process where Google checks that your app is legitimate and handles user data responsibly.

This is a more involved process. Google typically takes 4–6 weeks to complete verification and requires specific documentation. It is the right path only if you do not want to pay for Google Workspace or already have a strong reason to keep using a personal Gmail address.

What is required before you can apply

  • A publicly accessible homepage for the platform — a website that describes what the app does.
  • A Privacy Policy page on your domain that explains what data is collected and how it is used. This is mandatory for any app that accesses Gmail or Calendar on behalf of users.
  • Domain ownership verification — the homepage and privacy policy must be on a domain you own, verified in Google Search Console.
  • A clear description of why your app needs Calendar and Gmail access, written for Google's review team.
  • For Gmail send scope: Google may also require a YouTube video demonstrating your app using the Gmail permission (this is required for sensitive scopes).

Step-by-step process

1
Set up your domain and public pages

You need a live website with at minimum:

  • A homepage (e.g. https://yourpractice.com) describing the platform.
  • A Privacy Policy page (e.g. https://yourpractice.com/privacy) that mentions Google Calendar and Gmail access specifically. Free privacy policy generators such as Termly or PrivacyPolicies.com can help produce a compliant template.
2
Verify domain ownership in Google Search Console
  1. Go to search.google.com/search-console.
  2. Add your domain and follow the verification steps (usually adding a TXT record to DNS).
  3. Once verified, go back to the Cloud Console → OAuth consent screen → Branding and add the verified domain under Authorised domains.
3
Fill in all Branding fields
Branding

In the OAuth consent screen Branding section, complete all fields — Google requires these to be filled before you can submit for verification:

  • App name
  • App logo (a 120×120px image)
  • App homepage link
  • App privacy policy link
  • App terms of service link (optional but recommended)
  • Authorised domain (your verified domain from step 2)
4
Record a demonstration video (for Gmail scope)

Google requires a video for apps requesting sensitive scopes like gmail.send. The video must show:

  • A working demo of the app using the Gmail permission (e.g. sending a password reset email).
  • Where the app asks the user for permission (the OAuth consent screen).
  • How the app uses the data (what the sent email looks like).

Upload the video to YouTube (unlisted is fine) and have the URL ready for the submission form.

5
Submit for verification
Verification centre
  1. In the OAuth consent screen left menu, click Verification centre.
  2. Click Prepare for verification or Submit for verification.
  3. Fill in the form — you will be asked to explain why each scope is needed. Be specific: for Calendar, explain that you create appointment events on behalf of the platform; for Gmail, explain that you send password reset emails to registered users.
  4. Paste your YouTube demo video URL where asked.
  5. Submit.
6
Wait for Google's review

Google typically takes 4–6 weeks to complete the review. They may email you with questions or requests for additional information. Once approved, the app status changes from "Testing" to "In production" and refresh tokens no longer expire.

During the review period, continue regenerating the refresh token every 7 days as normal. Nothing changes on the server side — only the token expiry behaviour changes after approval.
After verification: the app moves to "In production" status. Refresh tokens issued from this point forward do not expire. You can also remove test users — any Google account can now authorise the app.

Option 1 pros and cons

  • Done in hours, not weeks
  • Professional sender domain
  • No Google review process
  • Includes a proper business email
  • Ongoing cost (~$6/month)
  • Requires a custom domain

Option 2 pros and cons

  • No subscription cost
  • Works with any Gmail address
  • 4–6 week wait for review
  • Requires privacy policy, homepage, video
  • Google can reject the application
  • Sender is still a Gmail address
Practical recommendation: For a clinical platform handling patient appointments, Option 1 is the right call. A custom domain email (appointments@yourpractice.com) is significantly more trustworthy to patients than a Gmail address, and the $6/month cost is negligible compared to the time and effort of Option 2. Most professional therapy practices already have or are planning to have a domain-based email for general correspondence anyway.
